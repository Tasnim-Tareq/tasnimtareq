Opening Story

Why materials, and why now

Growing up in Bangladesh, I witnessed a country striving to meet rapidly increasing energy demand while remaining heavily dependent on finite fossil fuel resources. Today, nearly 55–67% of the country's electricity still comes from non-renewable sources, primarily natural gas reserves projected to decline sharply within the coming decade. This is more than an energy statistic; it is a challenge that will shape the future of millions of lives.

That realization became the foundation of my research journey.

Rather than viewing energy as solely an engineering problem, I became fascinated by the materials that make energy conversion possible: why one material outperforms another, how microscopic defects shape macroscopic performance, and how subtle atomic changes fundamentally alter electronic and optical behavior.

This curiosity carried me from photovoltaic device simulation into computational materials science, where every project became another step toward one larger question: how can we design better materials to build better energy technologies?

Academic Journey

From asking "how" to "why"

UNDERGRADUATE

Learning to ask questions

Rather than simply learning existing technologies, I became interested in the physical mechanisms governing photovoltaic devices. My research centered on computational investigation of earth-abundant, lead-free perovskite solar cells using SCAPS-1D, spanning carrier transport, interface engineering, defect physics, device optimization, and semiconductor behavior. The work produced three peer-reviewed journal publications and established my foundation in computational research.

SCAPS-1D Defect Physics Device Optimization Lead-Free Perovskites
MASTER'S

Looking deeper

My master's work shifted from device-level optimization toward understanding materials at a more fundamental level.

Thesis — Metal-to-Semiconductor Transition in NaWO₃ via Nb Doping: Electronic and Optical Insights for Solar Cell Applications
"How can we improve a solar cell?" (2020–2023) → "Why does changing one atom change an entire material?" (2024–present)

Through density functional theory (DFT) calculations using VASP, I investigated how Nb doping reshapes the electronic and optical properties of NaWO₃, deepening my understanding of electronic structure engineering and material functionality.
